# Directory of Free Sources for Reviewing K-12 EdTech Accessibility (WCAG 2.1, Assistive Technology, and Disability Support)

## TL;DR
- This report compiles **41 free, publicly accessible sources** — spanning disability advocacy nonprofits, assistive-technology organizations, accessibility-auditor communities, special-education periodicals, parent/practitioner communities, and tool-review databases — that can inform a directory of K-12 EdTech products' WCAG 2.1 compliance and disability support.
- The single most authoritative cluster for your specific goal is the **CAST/AEM Center, WebAIM, and the National Center on Accessible Digital Educational Materials & Instruction (NCADEMI)**, which directly address WCAG, VPAT/ACR evaluation, and EdTech procurement for K-12 — paired with **Common Sense Education, BridgingApps, and TechMatrix** for product-level reviews.
- Caveats matter: most sources do **not** publish per-product WCAG 2.1 conformance audits (those live in vendor VPATs/ACRs); instead they offer expert frameworks, accessibility feature analysis, or community reviews, so the directory will need to triangulate across several source types.

## Key Findings
- **No single free database rates the named EdTech products (i-Ready, BrainPOP, Lexia, Newsela, etc.) against WCAG 2.1 line by line.** WCAG-specific conformance information is generally found in vendor-published VPATs/ACRs; the free sources below provide the evaluation lenses, accessibility-feature reviews, and disability-specific commentary needed to interpret and supplement those documents.
- **The strongest WCAG-anchored sources are nonprofits and federally funded technical-assistance centers** — CAST/AEM Center, NCADEMI, WebAIM, and the W3C Web Accessibility Initiative — which define accessibility standards and teach buyers how to vet products.
- **Product-level review coverage comes from a different tier** — Common Sense Education, BridgingApps, TechMatrix, and Closing The Gap — which evaluate apps for learning value and accessibility features (though often without explicit WCAG 2.1 conformance claims).
- **Disability-specific organizations add depth** for particular WCAG concern areas: American Foundation for the Blind (visual), DCMP/National Deaf Center (hearing), Understood/NCLD/LD OnLine/Reading Rockets (learning and cognitive), and ADDitude (ADHD).
- Several sources are **in transition or have caveats** (AEM Center became "Accessibility at CAST" in October 2024; Understood's "Tech Finder" review tool appears discontinued; Common Sense Education's EdTech review program ran 2013–2025 and, as of January 2026, paused publishing/updating reviews — with review pages slated for removal in February 2026; PATINS' Indiana DOE services concluded June 30, 2024). These remain valuable but should be cited with status notes.

## Details — Source Directory

### Category 1: Accessibility standards bodies & WCAG-focused nonprofits

**1. WebAIM (Web Accessibility In Mind)**
- URL: https://webaim.org/
- Type: Nonprofit (based at Utah State University's Institute for Disability Research, Policy & Practice)
- Relevance: The leading free resource for WCAG conformance; publishes the widely used "WCAG 2 Checklist," articles on VPAT/ACR use in purchasing, a contrast checker, and the WebAIM Screen Reader User Survey. (Survey #10, conducted December 2023–January 2024 with 1,539 valid responses, found "in 2024 the usage of both are again nearly the same at 41% for JAWS and 38% for NVDA.") Directly applicable to evaluating EdTech against WCAG 2.1 AA.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis.

**2. W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)**
- URL: https://www.w3.org/WAI/
- Type: International standards body
- Relevance: Authors WCAG itself; maintains the official "Web Accessibility Evaluation Tools List" (free tools for testing EdTech against WCAG 2.1/2.2).
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis / authoritative standards.

**3. The A11Y Project**
- URL: https://www.a11yproject.com/
- Type: Community-driven open-source initiative
- Relevance: Free, practical WCAG-referenced accessibility checklist and curated resource hub used by developers and auditors; useful for building an evaluation rubric for EdTech tools.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert/community analysis.

**4. Deque University (Deque Systems) — free resources**
- URL: https://dequeuniversity.com/resources/
- Type: Accessibility vendor with a free public resource library
- Relevance: Free WCAG 2.1/2.2 explainers, web accessibility checklist, and axe open-source testing tools usable to audit EdTech interfaces. (Full courses are paid; the resources page is free.)
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis/tools.

**5. TPGi — free accessibility resources & Colour Contrast Analyser**
- URL: https://www.tpgi.com/
- Type: Accessibility consultancy (Vispero) with free tools/blog
- Relevance: Free Colour Contrast Analyser (desktop), ARC Toolkit, and blog content relevant to WCAG testing of educational interfaces.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis/tools.

### Category 2: K-12-specific accessible-materials & EdTech accessibility centers

**6. AEM Center at CAST (National Center on Accessible Educational Materials)**
- URL: https://aem.cast.org/
- Type: Nonprofit technical-assistance center (CAST); OSEP-funded through Sept. 2024, now operating as "Accessibility at CAST"
- Relevance: Central K-12 authority on accessible educational materials and technologies; offers the "AEMing for Access" series on vetting tools against POUR/WCAG, procurement guidance ("Five Things Educators Can Do to Buy Accessible"), and the AEM Navigator/Pilot tools.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis.

**7. CAST**
- URL: https://www.cast.org/
- Type: Nonprofit; originator of Universal Design for Learning (UDL)
- Relevance: UDL framework underpins accessible EdTech design and evaluation; foundational for assessing cognitive/learning-disability support.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis.

**8. NCADEMI (National Center on Accessible Digital Educational Materials & Instruction)**
- URL: https://ncademi.org/
- Type: OSEP-funded technical-assistance center (successor to AEM's OSEP-funded TA role)
- Relevance: Its "Accessible EdTech" resources directly address ADA Title II, WCAG, and how to incorporate accessibility into EdTech procurement (RFPs, scoring matrices, VPAT/ACR review) — squarely on-target for your directory.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis.

**9. NIMAC / Louis (American Printing House for the Blind)**
- URL: https://louis.aph.org/
- Type: Nonprofit/federally funded accessible-media database
- Relevance: The Louis database "contains information on accessible materials produced by over 60 organizations throughout the United States" in braille, large print, audio, and electronic file format, and "also includes information from the national repositories of the NIMAC, Bookshare, and Learning Ally" — roughly 414,657 titles in total.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Database/directory.

### Category 3: Assistive-technology organizations & databases

**10. Assistive Technology Industry Association (ATIA)**
- URL: https://www.atia.org/
- Type: Nonprofit industry association
- Relevance: AT resources, webinars, and the open-access peer-reviewed journal "Assistive Technology Outcomes & Benefits (ATOB)"; useful for evidence on AT effectiveness and AT/EdTech interoperability standards.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis/research.

**11. TechMatrix**
- URL: http://www.techmatrix.org/
- Type: Free AT/educational-technology product database (National Center for Technology Innovation, operated by American Institutes for Research; originally OSEP-funded)
- Relevance: Searchable database of ~400 assistive and educational technology products matched to IDEA disability category, grade level, subject area, and accessibility features; includes "Ask the Tech Expert" answers and research citations. (Note: underlying federal funding has ended; confirm the live site loads before relying on it.)
- Reviews vs. analysis: Both (expert-reviewed product info + comparison tools).

**12. BridgingApps (Easterseals Greater Houston)**
- URL: https://bridgingapps.org/
- Type: Nonprofit program / parent-professional community
- Relevance: Free App Search Tool with a database of apps trialed and reviewed by therapists, special-education teachers, and special-needs users; organized by skill rather than diagnosis. Strong for disability-relevant app reviews.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Both (community + expert reviews).

**13. Closing The Gap**
- URL: https://www.closingthegap.com/resource-directory/
- Type: AT periodical / resource directory
- Relevance: Free Resource Directory of 2,000+ AT software/hardware products for children and adults. Note: directory listings are vendor-supplied descriptions, "not product reviews"; full magazine/training requires membership.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Directory (vendor descriptions).

**14. Explore AT / AT3 Center**
- URL: https://exploreat.net/
- Type: Federally funded clearinghouse for state AT programs
- Relevance: Organizes AT by activity and disability; links to TechMatrix, The Arc's Tech Toolbox, Unified Listing, and each state's AT Program (free device demos/loans).
- Reviews vs. analysis: Directory/expert analysis.

**15. Assistive Technology Internet Modules (ATIM)**
- URL: https://atinternetmodules.org/
- Type: Nonprofit/educational (OCALI)
- Relevance: 50+ free self-paced modules on understanding, selecting, and implementing AT, including a module on navigating Accessible Educational Materials (AEM).
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis/training.

**16. The Arc's Tech Toolbox**
- URL: https://www.thearc.org/
- Type: Disability advocacy nonprofit (intellectual & developmental disabilities)
- Relevance: A place to find, share, rate, and review technology for people with intellectual or developmental disabilities — one of the few community-rating resources for the IDD/cognitive-disability audience.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Community reviews.

### Category 4: Learning-disability, ADHD & cognitive-support organizations

**17. Understood.org**
- URL: https://www.understood.org/
- Type: Nonprofit (501(c)(3); founded with 15 partners including NCLD)
- Relevance: Leading free resource for learning and thinking differences; publishes expert AT guidance (e.g., "free assistive technology tools on the web," questions to ask when choosing AT), accessibility-feature explainers, and parent/educator articles. (Its standalone "Tech Finder" review tool appears discontinued/redirected; the AT article hub remains.)
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis (+ community groups).

**18. National Center for Learning Disabilities (NCLD)**
- URL: https://ncld.org/
- Type: Advocacy nonprofit
- Relevance: Policy and advocacy on learning disabilities and digital accessibility in education; founding partner of Understood.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis/advocacy.

**19. LD OnLine**
- URL: https://www.ldonline.org/
- Type: Public-media educational service (WETA)
- Relevance: Long-running free resource on learning disabilities and ADHD with articles, expert columns, forums, and a resource directory; includes AT overviews for LD.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis (+ forums).

**20. Reading Rockets**
- URL: https://www.readingrockets.org/
- Type: Public-media literacy initiative (WETA)
- Relevance: Free expert articles and curated app lists on AT for reading/LD (e.g., "Assistive Technology for Kids with Learning Disabilities") and autism app lists; strong on reading/cognitive support.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis/curated lists.

**21. ADDitude Magazine**
- URL: https://www.additudemag.com/
- Type: Periodical (ADHD/LD focus)
- Relevance: Free expert-written roundups of assistive technology apps and extensions for students with ADHD and learning disabilities, often mapped to specific challenges (dyslexia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia).
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis.

**22. The Shadow Project**
- URL: https://www.shadow-project.org/
- Type: Nonprofit
- Relevance: Publishes accessible-technology guidance for dyslexia, ADHD, and autism, including formatting/AT recommendations relevant to cognitive-disability support in EdTech.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis.

### Category 5: Disability-specific (vision, hearing, autism)

**23. American Foundation for the Blind (AFB)**
- URL: https://www.afb.org/edtech
- Type: National nonprofit (vision loss)
- Relevance: Its "Accessible Ed Tech Resources" and Access & Engagement research directly document EdTech accessibility barriers for blind/low-vision students; publishes digital-inclusion toolkits for families, teachers, administrators, and EdTech developers, and offers vendor consulting.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis/research (+ AccessWorld product evaluations).

**24. AccessWorld (AFB)**
- URL: https://www.afb.org/aw/
- Type: Free online technology magazine (AFB)
- Relevance: Reviews/evaluations of technology for people who are blind or visually impaired, including educational tools.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert reviews.

**25. Bookshare (Benetech)**
- URL: https://www.bookshare.org/
- Type: Nonprofit accessible e-book library (U.S. Dept. of Education-funded)
- Relevance: "The world's largest library of ebooks for people who read differently" — nearly 1.4 million titles, supported by partnerships with over 820 U.S. and international publishers; free for qualifying U.S. students with print disabilities. Useful as both a tool and a model of accessible-materials delivery.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Service/tool (with help-and-learning articles).

**26. Learning Ally**
- URL: https://learningally.org/
- Type: Nonprofit (human-read audiobooks; formerly Recording for the Blind & Dyslexic)

**27. Described and Captioned Media Program (DCMP)**

Publishes the free "Captioning Key" and "Description Key" standards used to evaluate media accessibility.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Both (expert standards + evaluated media library).

**28. National Deaf Center on Postsecondary Outcomes (NDC)**
- URL: https://nationaldeafcenter.org/
- Type: OSEP-funded technical-assistance center (University of Texas at Austin)
- Relevance: Evidence-based accessibility resources for deaf/hard-of-hearing learners; created the ASL-accessible educational game "Deafverse." Skews transition/postsecondary but useful for deaf-accessibility lens.
- Reviews vs. analysis: Expert analysis.

**29. Autism Speaks — Technology and Autism**

- Relevance: Curated AT/AAC guidance hub for autism, including expert advice on choosing communication apps/devices.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Build the evaluation backbone (do first).** Anchor the directory's WCAG 2.1 methodology in the standards/TA cluster: WebAIM's WCAG 2 Checklist, W3C-WAI's evaluation tools list, and NCADEMI/AEM Center procurement guidance. Use these to define what "accessible" means per product and to standardize how you record VPAT/ACR findings. Benchmark to change your approach: if a vendor VPAT claims "100% WCAG 2.1 AA," treat that as a red flag (per Khan Academy/Perkins guidance that full conformance is extremely rare) and require an independent third-party ACR.

**Stage 2 — Layer in product-level review sources.** For each of your named products (i-Ready, BrainPOP, Lexia, Newsela, etc.), pull (a) Common Sense Education learning + privacy reviews (capture these now, given the February 2026 removal of review pages — though privacy ratings persist), (b) BridgingApps and TechMatrix entries where available, and (c) the vendor's own VPAT/ACR. Where these disagree, note the conflict explicitly in the directory rather than picking one.

**Stage 3 — Add disability-specific lenses.** For each product, cross-reference the relevant specialist source by WCAG concern area: AFB/AccessWorld (vision/screen-reader), DCMP + National Deaf Center (captioning/hearing), Understood + LD OnLine + Reading Rockets + ADDitude (cognitive/learning/ADHD), and The Arc's Tech Toolbox (intellectual/developmental). This directly satisfies the assistive-technology, cognitive-disability, and learning-disability dimensions of your brief.

**Stage 4 — Capture community/parent signal.** Use BridgingApps community reviews, Common Sense teacher reviews, Wrightslaw, and CEC for practitioner/parent perspectives that surface real-world accessibility friction not captured in audits.

**Thresholds that should change your sourcing:** If a source has not been updated recently (e.g., Common Sense's review pages, scheduled for removal in February 2026, or a discontinued tool like Understood's Tech Finder), downgrade it to "archival" and prioritize a currently maintained equivalent. If you find a free source that publishes per-product WCAG 2.1 conformance results (rare), promote it to a primary source.

## Caveats
- **WCAG-specific, per-product conformance data is scarce in free sources.** Most listed sources provide frameworks, feature analysis, or learning/privacy reviews — not line-by-line WCAG 2.1 audits. Authoritative conformance claims usually come from vendor VPATs/ACRs, which should ideally be third-party-verified.
- **Registration/membership nuances:** A few sources are free but gated by registration (THE Journal, eSchool News) or have free + paid tiers (Closing The Gap directory is free; its magazine/training are membership-based; Deque's resources are free but courses are paid). None of the core listed sources require payment to access their primary free content.
- **Transition/status risks:** AEM Center became "Accessibility at CAST" (Oct. 2024) with OSEP TA functions moving to NCADEMI; PATINS' Indiana DOE services ended June 30, 2024 (site still live); Understood's "Tech Finder" appears discontinued/redirected; Common Sense Education's review program ran 2013–2025 and paused as of January 2026 with review pages slated for February 2026 removal; TechMatrix's federal funding has ended (verify the live site before relying on it). All remain useful but should be cited with current-status notes.
- **Source-quality variance:** Vendor blogs (e.g., accessibility-consultancy sites, text-to-speech companies) and listicles often mix genuine guidance with marketing; prioritize the nonprofit, federally funded, and standards-body sources for authoritative claims.
- This directory reflects sources verifiable as of June 2026; URLs and program statuses should be re-checked periodically.
